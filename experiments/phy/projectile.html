Watch the basketball. What shape is its path? In the ideal model, that curve is called a parabola — and you are about to understand why.
You shoot. It curves. Why?
Your arm gives the ball its initial velocity at launch. After release, ignoring air resistance, only gravity acts — so the horizontal speed stays constant while gravity pulls the ball downward at 9.81 m/s² every second. These two independent motions combine to trace a curved path called a parabola.

What is Projectile Motion?
When you throw something and only gravity acts on it, it is an ideal projectile. Its path is a parabola — a smooth curve that is symmetric only when launch and landing heights are the same.
Football kicks, basketball shots, rockets, water from a hose — all follow the same curve.
The Two Key Formulas
How far? → Range (flat ground, no drag)
R = v₀² · sin(2θ) / g
How high? → Max Height
H = v₀² · sin²(θ) / (2g)
v₀ = launch speed (m/s)θ = launch angleg = 9.81 m/s² on Earth
Why 45° gives maximum range — on flat ground with no air resistance: sin(2×45°) = sin(90°) = 1, the largest value sin can ever have. Any other angle gives a smaller sin(2θ) and a shorter range. In real sport, air drag and launch height can shift the ideal angle slightly below 45°.
